# Framework: The Common Enemy
> Source: Brand Brain Workshop Part 1; positioning theory
> Use for: brand conviction, content angles, community building

---

## The Core Idea

Strong positioning names not just who you serve and what you do, but what you stand against.

The enemy does not have to be a competitor. It can be:
- A behavior that is accepted as normal but is actively harming your client
- A belief that sounds reasonable but is wrong
- An institution or industry practice that your client is already frustrated by
- A shortcut that produces the opposite of the desired result

---

## Famous Examples

- **Apple** opposed corporate conformity and the beige-box mentality of big tech
- **Liquid Death** opposed plastic wellness culture and the idea that healthy has to be boring
- **CrossFit** opposed complacency and the comfortable gym experience that produces no results
- **Basecamp** opposed the culture of overwork and the always-on startup mythology

In each case, the enemy is not a competitor. It is a way of thinking that the brand's ideal customer already dislikes.

---

## How to Find Your Enemy

Ask:
1. What is the dominant approach in your industry that you believe is wrong or incomplete?
2. What do your best clients complain about before they find you?
3. What would you tell a friend not to do if they had the problem you solve?
4. What are people wasting money on that makes the actual problem worse?

---

## How to Use It

The enemy shows up in:
- **Content hooks:** "Most [category] programs do X. Here's why that makes the problem worse."
- **Positioning statement:** "Unlike [alternative that embodies the enemy behavior]..."
- **Community culture:** "We don't do X here. We do Y."
- **Sales conversation:** "What have you tried before? [They name the enemy.] Here's why that didn't work."
