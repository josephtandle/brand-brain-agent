# Framework: Category Design & POV
> Source: Christopher Lochhead, Dave Peterson, Al Ramadan — "Play Bigger" (2016)
> Also: Lochhead's podcast "Category Pirates" (2020+)
> Use for: brand differentiation in AI era, thought leadership positioning, content strategy

---

## The Core Premise

Don't compete in a category. Design a new one.

The company that names and frames a new category tends to capture approximately 76% of category economics. Not the best product — the category designer. Salesforce didn't win CRM — they designed "Software as a Service." Airbnb didn't win hotels — they designed "home sharing." Uber didn't win taxis — they designed "ridesharing."

In the AI era where tools are commoditized, the brand that defines a new way of thinking about the problem owns the conversation before it starts.

---

## The Category POV Structure

A Category POV is NOT a positioning statement. It operates at a higher level — it is a narrative about the world.

**Four components:**

### 1. The Broken World
What is wrong with the current dominant approach in your space? What pain does everyone accept as normal but shouldn't?

Frame it as a systemic problem, not a personal complaint. "The current approach to X is broken because..."

Example for example brand: "Most founders using AI are treating it as a productivity bolt-on — a collection of disconnected tools that speeds up existing chaos. They're not building systems. They're building faster noise."

### 2. The Stakes of Inaction
What happens to someone who doesn't change? Be specific. What does their world look like in 12 months if the broken world continues?

This is not fear-mongering — it is clarifying. The best Category POV makes the cost of inaction undeniably clear.

### 3. The Vision
What does the world look like when the problem is solved? Describe the future state in vivid, specific terms. Not features — a world.

This is the aspirational pull. Members of the category join because they believe in this future and want to be part of building it.

### 4. The Solution Frame
Position your approach as the only logical response to this specific future. Not "here is our product" — "here is why our approach is the only approach that addresses the real problem."

This is where your positioning statement lives — but beneath a worldview, not above it.

---

## The Lightning Strike Strategy

Once a Category POV is defined, concentrate resources to establish it:

- **Air war**: thought leadership content, media coverage, podcast appearances, speaking
- **Ground war**: in-person events, community building, partner activation, customer success stories

The goal is to make your POV "the talk of the town" — so that when people in your space discuss the problem, they use your framing.

---

## The Category Queen/King Principle

In any well-defined category, one player captures disproportionate value. Not the best product — the clearest category definer.

This means: the brand that helps people understand the problem (Level 2-3 awareness work) often wins even if someone else builds a better solution later. Framing is stickier than features.

## How to Build a Category POV Document

**Template:**

```
[BRAND] Category POV — [DATE]

## The Problem Nobody Is Talking About
[The broken world — what everyone accepts as normal but shouldn't. 2-3 paragraphs.]

## What Happens If Nothing Changes
[The stakes of inaction — specific, vivid, honest. 1-2 paragraphs.]

## The Future We're Building Toward
[The vision — what the world looks like when this is solved. 2-3 paragraphs.]

## Why [APPROACH] Is the Only Logical Response
[The solution frame — why your specific method is the necessary answer to this specific future. 2-3 paragraphs.]

## The Category Name
[What do you call this new category? One phrase that didn't exist before.]
```

## The Category Name

Every new category needs a name. The name should:
- Be new (not borrowed from an existing category)
- Be descriptive enough that people immediately grasp the concept
- Be ownable — associated with your brand before anyone else

Examples: "Software as a Service," "Growth Hacking," "Inbound Marketing," "Intuitive Intelligence," "AI Operating System," "Done-With-You Automation."

---

## POV Is the Only Moat in the AI Era

Research finding (Greenbook, 2025): Traditional brand moats (data, proprietary tech, normative databases) have been eroded by AI. The only non-commoditizable differentiator is **philosophical stance** — a specific, disagreeable POV on how the world should work.

A strong POV looks like: "We believe consumers lie to themselves; we measure non-conscious emotional drivers."

A weak POV looks like: "Full-service agile research agency with AI tools for CPG."

The difference is courage. A strong POV requires being willing to be wrong, to exclude potential customers, to have an actual belief about how things should work.

**Brand differentiation now lives in what a brand refuses, not what it produces.**
