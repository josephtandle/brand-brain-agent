# Framework: Shadow Archetype Analysis
> Source: Carl Jung — Shadow concept; applied to Brand Archetypes by practitioners
> Use for: brand risk assessment, content quality control, avoiding unintended tone

---

## The Shadow in Jungian Terms

The Shadow is the unconscious aspect of personality — the parts that are repressed, denied, or unacknowledged. In individuals, shadow material leaks out under stress, fatigue, or pressure.

In brands, the shadow is the dark side of the dominant archetype that emerges when:
- Copy is rushed or unfocused
- The brand tries too hard to persuade
- The brand is defending itself or reacting
- AI generates content without strong enough voice constraints

Shadow content damages trust faster than bad copy because it feels like a betrayal. The audience knows who the brand is supposed to be — when the shadow leaks through, it creates cognitive dissonance.

## The Shadow Map (12 Archetypes)

| Archetype | Core gift | Shadow expression | Content warning signs |
|-----------|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Sage** | Wisdom, truth | Condescension, know-it-all, withholding | "Most people don't understand..." / dismissive tone |
| **Creator** | Innovation, self-expression | Perfectionism, paralysis, elitism | Endless caveats / "this isn't for everyone" overused |
| **Hero** | Courage, mastery | Arrogance, ruthlessness, win-at-all-costs | Hustle culture framing / grinding language |
| **Caregiver** | Nurturing, service | Martyrdom, guilt-tripping, enabling | "I sacrifice for you" tone / obligation language |
| **Magician** | Transformation, vision | Manipulation, charlatanism, smoke and mirrors | Vague transformation promises without evidence |
| **Rebel/Outlaw** | Revolution, disruption | Nihilism, self-destruction, pure shock value | Edgy for its own sake / no constructive alternative |
| **Lover** | Intimacy, passion | Obsession, possessiveness, manipulation | Too intense / boundary-crossing personal tone |
| **Jester** | Joy, play | Irresponsibility, cruelty, dismissiveness | Using humor to avoid the serious / mocking |
| **Ruler** | Order, leadership | Rigidity, tyranny, entitlement | Talking down to the audience / "you should" language |
| **Innocent** | Optimism, purity | Naivety, denial, avoidance of complexity | Toxic positivity / ignoring real pain |
| **Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | Rootlessness, escapism, commitment-phobia | No grounding / always the next thing |
| **Everyman** | Belonging, relatability | Mediocrity, people-pleasing, no conviction | Everything for everyone / no position taken |

## The Dual Archetype Tension

The most powerful brands operate with a primary and secondary archetype in deliberate tension. The tension creates depth and prevents the brand feeling one-dimensional.

**Examples:**
- **Apple**: Magician (transformation) + Creator (innovation). Tension: transformation implies disruption; creation implies originality. Together: "we transform the world by building things that have never existed."
- **Nike**: Hero (mastery) + Outlaw (rebellion). Tension: mastery implies working within rules; rebellion implies breaking them. Together: "win by your rules, not theirs."
- **Patagonia**: Caregiver (stewardship) + Rebel (anti-corporate). Tension: caring implies nurturing existing structures; rebellion implies tearing them down. Together: "we care so much we're willing to disrupt our own industry."

**The example brand dual archetype:**
- **Primary**: Sage (wisdom from experience, real results, proof-based teaching)
- **Secondary**: Creator (builds real things, every session produces something, hands-on creation)
- **Tension**: Sage implies knowing the answers; Creator implies discovering through making. Together: "I know the way because I've built it myself — and I'll build it with you until you know it too."

**Latent third**: Magician (AI + Intuition worldview, Creator's Round Table, the transformation that happens when both forces align). This archetype can emerge in specific content contexts without conflicting with the primary.

## How to Use in Content Review

**Step 1 — Identify the primary archetype**
Read the project's BRAND-BRAIN.md, Section 4 (Brand Archetypes).

**Step 2 — Flag shadow expressions**
Read the content. Highlight any sentence that matches the shadow warning signs for the primary archetype.

**Step 3 — Assess severity**
- Minor: slight edge toward the shadow; a rewrite of one sentence fixes it
- Moderate: the tone of the piece activates the shadow; structural rewrite needed
- Severe: the entire piece embodies the shadow; do not publish

**Step 4 — Rewrite toward the gift**
Shadow expression usually appears when the copy is reaching for an effect it hasn't earned. The fix is almost always: be more specific, tell a real story, remove the persuasion pressure.

**Common fix patterns:**
- Condescension (Sage shadow) → Add a personal admission. "I got this wrong too before I..."
- Arrogance (Hero shadow) → Shift credit to the client. "What the client built in week two..."
- Manipulation (Magician shadow) → Ground the transformation claim in a specific proof point.
- Martyrdom (Caregiver shadow) → Remove all "for you" language; let the work speak.
